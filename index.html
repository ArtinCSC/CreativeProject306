I. Mock Trial Description


  1. Background/Scenario:

    • Ethan Turner, a customer of MacRonalds, is suing MacRonalds, for the injuries suffered from the accident that occurred in the drive-thru. Mr. Turner wants MacRonalds to remove their robot services from the drive-thru until the bugs are fixed.

      MacRonalds, a fast food chain, has recently come up with a new way to serve drive-thru items with robots. Ethan Turner, a customer who wanted to try out the new technology, visited MacRonalds and ordered a sandwich, fries and hot coffee. After giving the order, Mr. Turner drove up to the window and handed a 20-dollar bill as payment to the robot. The robot detected the hand and extended Mr. Turner the meal with the hot coffee. With his hand full, Mr. Turner was unable to hold the meal and this resulted to the hot coffee spilling on him, causing second-degree burns.

      Since MacRonalds new technology had bugs, and was not safe to be in service, Mr. Turner strongly believes that this service should be removed until all the bugs are fixed and it is safe to be used. Mr. Turner is suing MacRonalds for the defect in the system which caused a second-degree burn on his hand. On the contrary, MacRonalds wants to defend its new technology by proving the contributory negligence of Mr. Turner, the plaintiff.


  2. Legal Basis for Trial:

    • This particular case involves the victim Ethan Turner and the fast-food mogul MacRonalds. The plaintiff Ethan Turner, who suffered third-degree burns at the receiving end of the miscalculation, insinuated from the automated drive-thru robot, is suing the defendant MacRonalds on the grounds of Negligence and is demanding a preliminary injunction on further usage of the robot until advancement in the technology is made. The defendant must disprove the negligence allegations and show that the plaintiff had violated contributory negligence. Both Negligence and Contributory Negligence have 5 sub-elements that must be proved as outlined in the Virginia statute. However, contributory negligence under Virginia law follows the doctrine of pure contributory negligence which means that if the plaintiff is even so much as one percent at fault, he/she will have no case. As an effective means to distribute the workload of the team and the balance of the law for contributory negligence, only one statute will be covered, Duty, under contributory negligence.
