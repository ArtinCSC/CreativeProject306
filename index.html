Turner vs. MacRonalds



I. Mock Trial Description


  1. Background/Scenario:

    • Ethan Turner, a customer of MacRonalds, is suing MacRonalds, for the injuries suffered from the accident that occurred in the drive-thru. Mr. Turner wants MacRonalds to remove their robot services from the drive-thru until the bugs are fixed.

      MacRonalds, a fast food chain, has recently come up with a new way to serve drive-thru items with robots. Ethan Turner, a customer who wanted to try out the new technology, visited MacRonalds and ordered a sandwich, fries and hot coffee. After giving the order, Mr. Turner drove up to the window and handed a 20-dollar bill as payment to the robot. The robot detected the hand and extended Mr. Turner the meal with the hot coffee. With his hand full, Mr. Turner was unable to hold the meal and this resulted to the hot coffee spilling on him, causing second-degree burns.

      Since MacRonalds new technology had bugs, and was not safe to be in service, Mr. Turner strongly believes that this service should be removed until all the bugs are fixed and it is safe to be used. Mr. Turner is suing MacRonalds for the defect in the system which caused a second-degree burn on his hand. On the contrary, MacRonalds wants to defend its new technology by proving the contributory negligence of Mr. Turner, the plaintiff.


  2. Legal Basis for Trial:

    • This particular case involves the victim Ethan Turner and the fast-food mogul MacRonalds. The plaintiff Ethan Turner, who suffered third-degree burns at the receiving end of the miscalculation, insinuated from the automated drive-thru robot, is suing the defendant MacRonalds on the grounds of Negligence and is demanding a preliminary injunction on further usage of the robot until advancement in the technology is made. The defendant must disprove the negligence allegations and show that the plaintiff had violated contributory negligence. Both Negligence and Contributory Negligence have 5 sub-elements that must be proved as outlined in the Virginia statute. However, contributory negligence under Virginia law follows the doctrine of pure contributory negligence which means that if the plaintiff is even so much as one percent at fault, he/she will have no case. As an effective means to distribute the workload of the team and the balance of the law for contributory negligence, only one statute will be covered, Duty, under contributory negligence.

    • To be held liable for Negligence, the plaintiff must demonstrate:

      1. The defendant owed a duty toward the plaintiff (i.e. reasonable care for others safety).

        • MacRonalds did not demonstrate proper care and safety for their customers. Potentially designing and implement robots to their restaurants that were not ready for public use yet.

      2. The defendant failed to act in a reasonable way, or breached its duty (for example, a driver was reckless or intoxicated).

        • A lack of supervision of the robots or proper testing before their release.

      3. The defendant's breach was the actual cause of another's injuries.

        • The robot’s failure to complete correct or intended actions.

      4. The defendant's breach was the proximate cause of the injuries (the defendant should have known that the breach would cause injury).

        • The robot should have realized the customer was not ready to accept the meal.

      5. The plaintiff suffered actual injuries, for which they may claim damages .

        • The robot had caused some form of damage to the plaintiff.

    • To be found innocent, the defendant must demonstrate:

      1. The sub-element of duty:

        • That the plaintiff was at least somewhat responsible for the cause of his their own injuries. Possible scenarios include a lack of awareness, inability to read instructions, improper grip, or failure to avoid injury.

        • That the plaintiff had not properly followed the instructions given by the self serve robot.

        • That they have completed all the care necessary to provide safety to their customer. For example, sufficient testing on the robot before using them in their store.

  4. Lexis/Nexis Case Bank:

    1. Wright v. Webb, 234 Va. 527 - This case brings forth the idea of duty of care. The owner did not have a duty of care for the customers without knowing the incident were to occur. In conclusion, the court overturned the trail for the invitee, and judgement for the owner. Comparing it to the case in hand, Turner v. MacRonalds, the concern of duty of care comes to play where the question lies weather the duty of MacRonalds comprises of dealing with incident during transaction.


    2. Tate v. Rice, 227 Va. 341 - This case brings forth the idea of duty of care. The owner did not have a duty of care for the customers without knowing the incident were to occur. In conclusion, the court affirmed the summary judgement for the landowner. Comparing it to the Turner v. MacRonalds, the duty of care overturns on the plaintiff, stating that MacRonalds is responsible for some things, and some, they are not.


    3. Smith v. Norfolk & W. Ry., 48 W. Va. 69 - This case was concerned with a breach of duty relating to the employer of the employee who assaulted a passenger of the train. The case concluded the employer is liable for the assault and battery because of the employee’s breach of duty.


    4. Atlantic Rural Exposition, Inc. v. Fagan, 195 Va. 13 - This case involves a racetrack owner and driver being sued by an invitee to the racetrack as a spectator. A wheel of a driver spun out of control and hit the invitee. However, due to the fact the vehicle was reasonably tested, the owners and driver of the racetrack did not breach their duty in causing harm to the plaintiff.


    5. Coleman v. Blankenship Oil Corp., 221 Va. 124 - This case reversed the trial court's original ruling of causation against the plaintiff. The case involved the injuries caused to the plaintiff from an automobile accident due to an oil spillage that was spilled by the defendant. Regarding to our case, this case (Coleman v. Blankenship Oil Corp.) can help show the jury that the plaintiff did not act in an imprudent way in the drive-thru. It could also favor the plaintiff in our case by showing how the lack of testing from MacRonalds ultimately caused the injuries.


    6. Norfolk & W. R. Co. v. Emmert, 83 Va. 640 This case reversed and annulled the judgment and verdict of the circuit court against the defendant. The case involved the injuries of an employee of a railroad company sustained by a defective caboose bumper and link. But the plaintiff being a car-coupler wasn't paying attention to the condition of the bumper. Regarding to our case (Norfolk & W. R. Co. v. Emmert) this shows that the customer is responsible for his own safety once operating through the drive-thru and is thus required to read through any and all instructions posted.


    7. Safeway Stores, Inc. v. Tolson, 203 Va. 13. - Regarding to our case (Turner v. MacRonalds), this case (Safeway Stores, Inc. v. Tolson, 203 Va. 13) helps the defender to show that they have done all the care required to provide safety to their customer as the court mentioned “that the burden of proving negligence had been on the customer and that the duty owed to the customer by the store had been one of reasonable care.”


    8. Tazewell Supply Co. v. Turner, 213 Va. 93. - Regarding to our case (Turner v. MacRonalds), this case (Tazewell Supply Co. v. Turner) helps the defendant (MacRonalds) to show that the plaintiff contributed in the negligence and his negligence act was the proximate cause. As if the plaintiff cannot show evidence of negligent of the defendant for properly testing the robot before using them in their store, and based on Virginia law of negligence there must be 100 percent negligent from the MacRonalds in order to Court decide in favor of the plaintiff.


    9. Shiflett v. M. Timberlake, Inc., 205 Va. 406.- Regarding to our case (Turner v. MacRonould), this case (Shiflett v. M. Timberlake, Inc.) can help the plaintiff show the jury that he as an invitee had a right to assume the premises were safe. Whether the hazard was so open that she should have observed it and was contributorily negligent in not doing so was for the jury to decide. MacRonalds should have considered all the possible actions that could become a hazard for their customers.


    10. Logan v. Montgomery Ward & Co., 216 Va. 425- Regarding a situation where a Manufacturer is liable for the malfunctions of its technology, in particular a stove. The company was not found to be negligent in the production of the stove and was found to be the victor in both district and appeals court. This could possibly bode well in favor of the defendant.


    11. Lewis v. Carpenter Co., 252 Va. 296- Another potential case in favor of the defendant. This case was in regards to the scenario on whether or not the plaintiff can prove that the incident would not have occurred had the truck driver exercised reasonable care? A similar argument can then be made with a robot in our situation.


    12. Carolina, C. & O. R. Co. v. Mullins, 207 Va. 207 - This case presents the plaintiff the opportunity to show some proof that the alleged wrongdoer must be the cause of an injury to succeed in proving damages. One of the main points to the case was the following statement: “Injury alone is not sufficient to support an action for damages arising from the alleged negligence of a defendant. There must be a concurrence of wrong and injury, and the negligence of an alleged wrongdoer must be the cause of an injury.” This case in particular discussed the matter where a homeowner was presented with the opportunity to showcase the damages negligence that had been conducted by the railroad company. Ultimately, the plaintiff failed to show this, but can be construed positively for the plaintiff if the main point can be shown successfully.


    13. Artrip v. E.E. Berry Equip.Co., 240 Va. 354 - This case reversed the original ruling of contributory negligence against the plaintiff. The case involved the injury caused to the plaintiff from falling due to a snowbank that was plowed by the defendant. But the duty of the plaintiff may not have been broken upon review after an appeal from the plaintiff.


    14. Litchford v. Hancock, 232 Va. 496- The case involves an auto collision where the plaintiff claimed the vehicle she hit was responsible for her injuries under grounds of negligence. The defendant argued the plaintiff was contributorily negligent in her duty of not maintaining proper distance and awareness of her surroundings and was found to be contributorily negligent.
